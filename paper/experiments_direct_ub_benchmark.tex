\section{Experiments: direct $[u,b]\to y$ baselines versus joint LFISTA}
\label{sec:exp_direct_ub}

This section complements Phase~0 (Section~\ref{sec:exp_synthetic}) with an \emph{end-to-end} comparison on the \textbf{same} synthetic generator and $(\mathrm{seed},\rho)$ protocol: several regressors predict $y$ directly from standardized $[u,b]$ with $b=My+\eta$, versus the PyTorch joint LFISTA map (\texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint}) trained in the LFISTA branch of the pipeline.
We first document the Phase~0-style regime ($\Psi=I$, Gaussian $M$), then two controlled ablations ($\Psi$ axis, then $M$ axis at DCT $\Psi$), ending with a structured DCT$+$subsample protocol (Section~\ref{sec:direct_ub_dct_subsample}) where the joint estimator becomes strongly competitive.
The initial goal is to show when a strong direct nonlinear map can outperform the structured path; the later subsections show when the opposite holds under a more SIR-CS-aligned measurement model.

\subsection{Protocol}
\label{sec:direct_ub_protocol}

We run \texttt{sir\_cs\_benchmark\_direct\_ub.py} with profile \texttt{direct\_ub\_lfista\_joint\_only}: seeds $\{7,13,\ldots,53\}$, $\rho\in\{0.20,0.30,0.40,0.50,0.60\}$, identical \texttt{make\_dataset} splits and measurement draws as the hybrid evaluation for each job.
Features are $[u,b]$ with independent Gaussian noise on $b$ at train/val/test; \texttt{StandardScaler} is fit on train $[u,b]$ only.

\paragraph{Methods.}
\texttt{ml\_only}: sklearn MLP on $u$ only (reference; flat in $\rho$).
\texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub}: MLP on scaled $[u,b]$ (same hidden layers and regularization defaults as the pipeline MLP baseline).
\texttt{pca\_regression\_ub}: PCA on $Y_{\mathrm{train}}$ only; latent dimension selected by validation; MLP maps $[u,b]$ to PCA scores then inverse-transforms to $y$.
\texttt{ae\_regression\_ub}: PyTorch autoencoder on standardized $Y_{\mathrm{train}}$; MLP maps $[u,b]$ to latent then decodes to $y$.
\texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint}: joint LFISTA training (\texttt{run\_lfista\_branch}); this run omits \texttt{hybrid\_fista}, \texttt{ml\_only\_torch}, and \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_frozen}.

\subsection{Psi-axis pilot: identity $\Psi$ versus DCT $\Psi$}
\label{sec:direct_ub_psi_ablation}

\paragraph{Protocol (isolated $\Psi$ axis).}
We re-run the same \texttt{direct\_ub\_lfista\_joint\_only} job tree with \texttt{--residual-basis dct} instead of the default \texttt{identity}:
same seeds, $\rho$ grid, Gaussian $M$, noise stds, splits, and the \emph{same} rule in code for drawing $\alpha^\star$; only $\Psi=\texttt{get\_basis}(N,\texttt{residual\_basis})$ changes, so that $y_{\mathrm{res}}=\Psi\alpha^\star$ lives in a different subspace of $\mathbb{R}^N$.
The identity reference is frozen run \texttt{20260422\_102236} ($\Psi=I$); the DCT run is \texttt{20260422\_175210} ($\Psi=\mathrm{DCT}$), produced by the same script version and stored under \texttt{outputs/direct\_ub\_psi\_ablation/dct/}.

\subsection{Structured protocol: DCT $\Psi$ and subsampled $M$}
\label{sec:direct_ub_dct_subsample}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
Sections~\ref{sec:direct_ub_table}--\ref{sec:direct_ub_psi_ablation} show that identity $\Psi$ with Gaussian $M$ favors direct $[u,b]\to y$ maps, and that DCT $\Psi$ with the \emph{same} Gaussian $M$ narrows the joint-versus-MLP gap but does not make \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} the best RMSE row at every $\rho$.
We now fix $\Psi=\mathrm{DCT}$ and change only the measurement operator to \emph{subsample} coordinates: $M$ picks $m$ distinct output indices (one-hot rows), which is closer to a ``partial observation'' reading of $b$ than a fully mixed dense Gaussian $M$.

\paragraph{Protocol (frozen run \texttt{20260422\_205913}).}
Same script and profile as before: \texttt{sir\_cs\_benchmark\_direct\_ub.py} \texttt{--joint-only}, \texttt{--residual-basis dct}, \texttt{--measurement-kind subsample}, ten seeds, $\rho\in\{0.20,\ldots,0.60\}$, same noise stds and splits; \texttt{build\_measurement\_matrix} uses random subsampling without replacement for the support of each row of $M$.
For comparison, the DCT$+$Gaussian run remains \texttt{20260422\_175210} (Section~\ref{sec:direct_ub_psi_ablation}).

\paragraph{Effect of subsampling $M$ at fixed DCT $\Psi$ (joint RMSE).}
Table~\ref{tab:joint_rmse_dct_gauss_vs_sub} isolates the $M$-axis: same $\Psi=\mathrm{DCT}$, same seeds and $\rho$ grid; only \texttt{measurement\_kind} changes from \texttt{gaussian} to \texttt{subsample}. The direct \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} row barely moves, whereas \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} drops sharply at medium--high $\rho$, which is why the head-to-head flips at $\rho=0.60$.

\paragraph{MAE (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_mae} and Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_curves}, center).}
This is the metric where \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} is strongest: at $\rho=0.20$ it is within $0.004$ of the best PCA row and already below \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} and \texttt{ae\_regression\_ub}; from $\rho=0.30$ onward it is the unique best method, with a widening margin through $\rho=0.60$.
The MAE curves therefore support the narrative that the joint map corrects structured residual error more systematically than end-to-end $[u,b]\to y$ regressors once enough coordinate measurements are available.

\paragraph{RMSE (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_rmse} and Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_curves}, left).}

\paragraph{Methodological interpretation.}
The progression identity$\Psi+$Gaussian $M$ $\to$ DCT$\Psi+$Gaussian $M$ $\to$ DCT$\Psi+$subsample $M$ mirrors increasingly structured hypotheses: a global background in $u$, a compressible innovation in $\Psi$, and partial linear observations $b=My+\eta$ that are less ``fully mixed'' than a dense Gaussian $M$.
Under the final protocol, the decomposition used by SIR-CS appears better aligned with the data-generating process than a single black-box $[u,b]\to y$ map, which is why \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} can lead strong baselines on key metrics at sufficiently high $\rho$ without abandoning the earlier benchmarks that document the opposite regime.

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
The benchmark with identity $\Psi$ and Gaussian $M$ (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_rmse}) illustrates a regime where direct maps win; the DCT$+$Gaussian ablation (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_psi_rmse_gap}) shows a controlled improvement from changing $\Psi$ only; the DCT$+$subsample protocol above identifies a \emph{structured} regime where \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} is superior on MAE over most of the $\rho$ grid and leads on RMSE/relative $\ell_2$ at the largest $\rho$ tested. This is evidence of a \textbf{regime of validity} for the SIR-CS joint estimator, not a claim of dominance on all generators or all measurement models.
A complementary \emph{pilot} sweep varies only the standard deviation of Gaussian noise $\eta$ in $b=My+\eta$ (fixed $\Psi=\mathrm{DCT}$, subsample $M$, same $\rho$ grid); reproduction and directory layout are in \texttt{docs/direct\_ub\_robustness\_measurement\_noise.txt}. The merged long-format table is \texttt{outputs/direct\_ub\_robustness/dct\_subsample/merged\_measurement\_noise\_long.csv}.
Frozen pilot arms with parity export: \texttt{noise\_0p01\_20260423\_140024} ($\mathrm{std}(\eta)=0.01$), \texttt{noise\_0p02\_20260423\_143832} ($0.02$), \texttt{noise\_0p04\_20260423\_150923} ($0.04$).

\subsubsection{Integrated reading: dashboards, gains, noise pilot, and conclusions}

\paragraph{Qualitative shift relative to earlier direct-$[u,b]$ regimes.}
Phase~0-style identity$\Psi+$Gaussian $M$ (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_rmse}) is a setting where strong global regressors from $[u,b]$ are difficult to beat. The DCT$+$subsample configuration changes the problem geometry enough that \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} is no longer only ``close'' to the best direct maps: on the ten-seed frozen run \texttt{20260422\_205913}, the error panels (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_curves}) show a coherent pattern---\textbf{MAE} separates earliest, \textbf{RMSE} and \textbf{relative $\ell_2$} cross later---consistent with a map $y\approx f(u)+\Psi\alpha$ being actively useful when $M$ is sparse and $\Psi$ compresses residuals.

\paragraph{MAE: strongest and earliest signal.}
Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_mae} makes the MAE story quantitative: at $\rho=0.20$, \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} ($0.221$) is within $0.004$ of the best PCA row and already below \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} and \texttt{ae\_regression\_ub}; from $\rho=0.30$ through $\rho=0.60$ it is the unique best method, with the end-point MAE ($0.102$ at $\rho=0.60$) well below the best direct rows ($0.145$--$0.152$). MAE is hard to ``hide behind'' because it weights all coordinates evenly; here it is the cleanest headline for structured recovery under partial linear measurements.

\paragraph{RMSE and relative $\ell_2$: crossover and scaling with $\rho$.}
RMSE is more conservative (Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_rmse}): \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} / \texttt{pca\_regression\_ub} lead at $\rho\in\{0.20,0.30,0.40\}$, the joint is tied near $\rho=0.50$, and it becomes the best RMSE at $\rho=0.60$ ($0.180$ vs.\ $0.188$ for \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub}). Relative $\ell_2$ (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_curves}, right) tracks the same story: the joint is not best at the lowest $\rho$, closes the gap by $\rho\approx 0.50$, and is lowest at $\rho=0.60$. The interpretation is not that the joint ignores heavy tails, but that \emph{it benefits more from additional coordinate measurements} once the structured path is aligned with the DGP---RMSE rewards occasional large errors, so leadership appears only when enough of $b$ is informative.

\paragraph{Gain panels vs.\ \texttt{ml\_only} (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_gains}).}
The gain plots (baseline $-$ method, positive is better) isolate how much each method improves over a $u$-only MLP. \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} achieves the largest MAE gain at every $\rho$, and its RMSE gain rises fastest with $\rho$, ending highest at $\rho=0.60$. Direct $[u,b]$ maps also improve over \texttt{ml\_only}, but the joint's curve is the one whose \emph{slope} vs.\ $\rho$ is steepest, which is the empirical signature we want for ``using $b$ well'' under this protocol.

\paragraph{Support F1 (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_sub_support}, left).}
Support F1 for the joint stays in a narrow band near $0.09$--$0.10$ with mild non-monotone variation. The ordering in Tables~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_rmse} and~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_mae} is therefore driven primarily by reconstruction errors, not by a spurious spike in support recovery. We treat support F1 as a \textbf{secondary} diagnostic here, not the headline metric.

\paragraph{Noise-on-$b$ pilot (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_noise_robust}, Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_noise_pilot_joint}, and Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_noise_pilot_parity}).}
The pilot uses \texttt{--robustness-lite} (three seeds; not the same Monte Carlo budget as run \texttt{20260422\_205913}) and three noise levels $\{0.01,0.02,0.04\}$ on $b$ with identical $\Psi$, subsample $M$, and $\rho$ grid. Table~\ref{tab:direct_ub_noise_pilot_joint} shows that both RMSE and MAE for \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} shift upward as $\mathrm{std}(\eta)$ increases at fixed $\rho$, while the decrease with $\rho$ at fixed noise remains monotone in this window. At $\rho=0.60$, RMSE moves from $0.174$ to $0.179$ across the noise arms---a small absolute change relative to the gap vs.\ direct baselines in the merged file. The qualitative ordering ``joint vs.\ \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} at high $\rho$'' is preserved across noise arms in that pilot. Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_noise_robust} plots RMSE vs.\ $\rho$ (joint only, one curve per noise std). Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_noise_pilot_parity} gives a pooled parity view at $\mathrm{std}(\eta)=0.02$: \texttt{mlp\_concat\_ub} and \texttt{pca\_regression\_ub} track the identity line tightly; \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} is competitive globally on this scatter while the \emph{regime story} (who leads by $\rho$) is still best read from Tables~\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_rmse} and~\ref{tab:direct_ub_noise_pilot_joint}. Full five-method dashboards and \texttt{parity\_pooled.npz} per arm remain under \texttt{outputs/direct\_ub\_robustness/.../runs/} per \texttt{docs/direct\_ub\_robustness\_measurement\_noise.txt}.

\paragraph{Conclusions for the paper's claim.}
The correct scientific statement is \textbf{conditional}: the proposed joint estimator can \textbf{lead} strong $[u,b]$ baselines on MAE over most of the $\rho$ grid tested and on RMSE/relative $\ell_2$ at the largest $\rho$, \emph{in this} DCT$+$subsample synthetic reference, while earlier rows of the experimental roadmap still document regimes where direct maps win. That is a \textbf{regime-of-validity} narrative, not a universal superiority claim; it is strengthened by (i) explicit ablations on $\Psi$ and $M$, (ii) aligned error metrics, and (iii) a small noise robustness pilot showing stable high-$\rho$ ordering under a bandwidth of $\eta$.

\paragraph{Optional extensions.}
A single follow-up axis (for example residual $k$ only, or an additional noise level) would further stress-test whether high-$\rho$ leadership is fragile to a one-dimensional synthetic knob; we leave that as optional work beyond the pilot above.

See \texttt{docs/direct\_ub\_m\_ablation.txt} for the exact shell protocol of the main M-axis comparison.

\subsubsection*{Takeaways}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Under DCT$+$subsample, \texttt{hybrid\_lfista\_joint} \textbf{beats} the best direct $[u,b]$ baselines on \textbf{MAE} from $\rho=0.30$ upward and on \textbf{RMSE} and \textbf{relative $\ell_2$} at the \textbf{largest} $\rho$ tested, while remaining competitive at $\rho=0.20$.
  \item The advantage is \textbf{not universal}: the Phase~0 identity benchmark and the DCT$+$Gaussian row still show regimes where direct maps lead; the contribution is an explicit \textbf{map of regimes} rather than a single winner everywhere.
  \item The experimental step from dense Gaussian $M$ to subsampled $M$ at fixed DCT $\Psi$ was \textbf{decisive} for competitiveness (Table~\ref{tab:joint_rmse_dct_gauss_vs_sub}), motivating this structured configuration as the primary ``SIR-CS-aligned'' synthetic reference going forward.
  \item A pooled parity view at $\mathrm{std}(\eta)=0.02$ on the pilot arm (Figure~\ref{fig:direct_ub_noise_pilot_parity}) complements Tables~\ref{tab:direct_ub_noise_pilot_joint}--\ref{tab:direct_ub_dct_subsample_rmse}: direct $[u,b]$ maps track identity tightly in the aggregate cloud, while the joint remains well calibrated without substituting for the $\rho$-by-$\rho$ head-to-head tables.
\end{enumerate}

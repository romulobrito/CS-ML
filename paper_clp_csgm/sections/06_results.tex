\section{Results}

\subsection{Cross-well low-data results}

Table~\ref{tab:crosswell_clp_vs_ae} compares CLP-CSGM Ridge against the
strongest direct baseline, AE \([u,b]\), for every low-data and sparse
measurement cell. CLP-CSGM Ridge wins in all nine cells. The relative RMSE gap
is modest at step 8 and \(\rho=0.05\), but becomes large as the low-data regime
becomes more severe, reaching improvements above 38\% for step 32 at
\(\rho=0.10\) and \(\rho=0.20\). Figure~\ref{fig:crosswell_clp_vs_ae}
visualizes the same pairwise comparison and shows that the advantage is not
confined to a single measurement ratio: within each training-size panel, the
CLP-CSGM curve remains below the AE curve across the sparse-measurement grid.

Figure~\ref{fig:crosswell_all_methods} places this pairwise comparison in the
full benchmark context. The direct MLP and PCA baselines become unstable in the
low-data cells, with errors far above the ML-only and AE baselines. This
behavior is informative but not the main evidence for the proposed method,
because these direct models are clearly fragile in this regime. The main
competitor is AE \([u,b]\), which uses the same autoencoder representation
family as a direct supervised latent regressor. CLP-CSGM Ridge consistently
stays below AE \([u,b]\) across the measurement ratios, so the gain is not an
artifact of comparing only against weak baselines; it is visible against the
strongest direct representation-learning baseline.

The overall ranking in Table~\ref{tab:crosswell_rank} shows the same pattern.

0.01397, compared with 0.01905 for AE \([u,b]\). This corresponds to a relative
gap of about 26.65\% against the strongest direct baseline.

\subsection{Real-well F03 GR-only results}

Table~\ref{tab:f03_results} reports the F03-4 GR-only porosity benchmark.
CLP-CSGM Ridge has the best mean RMSE across measurement ratios, 0.01360 versus
0.01398 for AE \([u,b]\). The gain is concentrated in the sparse-measurement
regime: CLP-CSGM Ridge is clearly better at \(\rho=0.20\) and \(\rho=0.30\).
At higher measurement ratios, AE \([u,b]\) becomes slightly better.
Figure~\ref{fig:f03_gr_only_rmse} makes this crossover explicit: the proposed
method is most useful when target-well measurements are scarce, whereas the
direct AE baseline benefits once more entries of \(b\) are available as input
features.

The MAE and relative-\(\ell_2\) curves in
Figs.~\ref{fig:f03_mae}--\ref{fig:f03_rell2} confirm that the RMSE behavior is

the sparse-to-moderate ratios, while the direct MLP baseline deteriorates as
more sparse-measurement features are added. This suggests that naively
concatenating \(b\) to \(u\) can inject high-variance features, whereas the
proposed method uses \(b\) only through the physical measurement residual.

measurements should be incorporated through a measurement model rather than only
as raw input features.

The qualitative diagnostics show how these aggregate gains appear in
reconstructed profiles. Figure~\ref{fig:f03_examples} shows representative test

Ridge, indicating better ordering of low and high porosity values. Finally,
Fig.~\ref{fig:f03_depth} reconstructs the test interval along depth and shows
that CLP-CSGM tracks the observed porosity trend without the high-frequency
instability of the direct MLP baseline.

This behavior is consistent with the design of CLP-CSGM. When measurements are
very sparse, the log-conditioned latent prior prevents the inverse problem from
fitting arbitrary sparse observations, while the measurement-consistency term
corrects the weak GR-only prior. When measurements become denser, a direct AE
baseline can use the \([u,b]\) feature vector effectively and the extra latent
optimization may offer less advantage.

\subsection{Ablation analysis}

The ablation study isolates the two structural components of CLP-CSGM Ridge:
the conditional latent prior and the sparse-measurement refinement. The
prior-only variant uses \(\hat y=G(h(u))\), without \(b\). The measurement-only
variant solves the CSGM objective with \(\lambda=0\), removing the
log-conditioned anchor. Table~\ref{tab:ablation_crosswell} and
Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation_crosswell} show that the full method improves over both
ablations in every cross-well low-data cell. The gain over prior-only ranges
from 6.4\% to 24.8\%, while the gain over measurement-only ranges from 39.4\%
to 61.3\%. This confirms that the cross-well result is not explained solely by
the decoder or solely by sparse measurements; the advantage comes from their
combination.

The real-well F03 GR-only ablation in Table~\ref{tab:ablation_f03} and

all ratios, with the largest gains in the sparse regime. The comparison with
AE \([u,b]\) remains regime-dependent: CLP-CSGM is better at \(\rho=0.20\) and
\(\rho=0.30\), while AE is slightly better at higher ratios.

Finally, we compare the Ridge latent prior against a more flexible MLP prior
using the same experimental grids, without repeating the structural ablations.
This is a prior-class sensitivity check rather than an architecture search.
Table~\ref{tab:prior_class_crosswell} shows that Ridge outperforms the MLP prior
in all nine cross-well cells, with RMSE reductions from 8.4\% to 33.9\%.
Table~\ref{tab:prior_class_f03} shows the same pattern on F03-4: Ridge is better
at every measurement ratio, with the largest gains in the sparse regime. These
results support the use of the low-capacity Ridge prior as the paper-facing
variant in the studied low-data settings. Figure~\ref{fig:prior_class_ridge_mlp}
summarizes this comparison visually: the Ridge curves remain below the MLP
curves on both datasets, indicating that the more flexible prior does not
improve the latent anchor under the available training data.
